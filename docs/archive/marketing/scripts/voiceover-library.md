### A1 — Why I Built Unit

**Length:** ~90 seconds | **Word count:** 243 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~97 seconds

**Visual notes:** Face-to-camera, gym setting or desk. Cut to screen recording of the app at the ghost-values moment. No music. No intro card.

---

I kept a paper notebook for about three years. Ring-bound, same format every session — exercise, sets, weight, reps. The page was already open when I walked up to the bar. I wrote the set in about two seconds. Then I put the pen down.

Every gym app I tried was slower than that. Not by a little — most of them had four or five taps before I could confirm a set. Strong, Hevy, Jefit. I tried them all. Some have good charts, some have rest timers, some have a decent exercise library. But none of them passed the notebook test on the gym floor.

The specific problem is that most apps are designed around the idea that you need guidance — a weight suggestion, a rep target, an AI telling you how close you are to your goal. I don't need that. I have a program. I just need to record what I actually did, fast, one-handed, with chalk on my hands, thirty seconds before the next set starts.

So I built Unit. The ghost value pre-fills last session's weight and reps. You tap once to confirm or you adjust if you went up. Haptic feedback. Timer auto-starts. Then you put your phone in your pocket and you lift.

That's it. No AI coach. No social feed. No weekly summary card asking if you "crushed it."

It's free to download. The core logging is free forever — I promised that early and I'm keeping it.

### A2 — What's Broken with Hevy and Strong

**Length:** ~60 seconds | **Word count:** 162 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~65 seconds

**Visual notes:** Face-to-camera with B-roll of the competitor UI (Hevy set-logging flow, Strong exercise selection). Cut to Unit's one-tap confirm. No music.

---

The set log in Hevy is four taps under good conditions. You open the exercise, you tap the weight field, you enter the weight, you tap the reps field, you enter reps, you tap Done, and then you're back. That's six interactions, not four — I miscounted the first time, which should tell you something.

Strong is better — three interactions to confirm a set — but still relies on you manually typing the number every time, because the weight from last session isn't pre-filled by default.

The problem isn't that these apps are bad. They're not. The problem is that both of them were designed to be looked at, and a gym logger needs to be used — one-handed, fatigued, under a bar.

Ghost values solve this. Your last session pre-fills. You either tap once to confirm, or you adjust the number if you went up. One interaction, not six. That's the whole design premise.

### A3 — Why Ghost Values Are the Only Progression UI You Need

**Length:** ~90 seconds | **Word count:** 248 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~99 seconds

**Visual notes:** Face-to-camera first 20s. Cut to screen recording showing ghost values pre-filling. Then close with face-to-camera. No music.

---

Every progression engine in every gym app works the same way. You log a set, the app looks at your history, and it decides what you should do next week. Usually it's a linear recommendation — you hit your target, the weight goes up. You miss it, the weight stays or drops.

The idea is reasonable. The execution is the problem.

Here's what actually happens under fatigue: you look at the suggested weight, and you don't trust it. Maybe you slept five hours. Maybe you're two hours into a session. Maybe you know your shoulder is off today and 87.5 kilograms is not going to happen. The "right" answer according to the app is not the right answer in your body right now.

So you override it. And you do that enough times that the app's model of your training diverges from your actual training. At that point the recommendation is noise — it's worse than nothing, because now you're spending mental energy either following bad advice or arguing with a screen.

Ghost values don't recommend anything. They show you what you did last time. You decide whether to match it, exceed it, or back off. That decision is yours — it should be yours — and it takes about one second of context to make.

The difference isn't between a "smart" app and a "dumb" app. It's between an app that respects your expertise and one that doesn't.

### A4 — Notebook vs Unit: Speed Test

**Length:** ~60 seconds | **Word count:** 156 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~62 seconds

**Visual notes:** Face-to-camera intro 5s. Cut to split-screen: hand writing in notebook with visible stopwatch vs Unit tap with overlay timer. Close on face-to-camera. No music.

---

The claim is that Unit is faster than a paper notebook. I'm going to show you the actual numbers.

Notebook: open the page, find the row, write the weight and reps, cap the pen. In my timing across ten sets in a real session, that averages around seven to nine seconds, depending on how messy the handwriting is getting toward the end.

Unit: ghost value is pre-filled from last session. One tap to confirm. Haptic feedback. Timer auto-starts. That's about 1.8 to 2.4 seconds, and the slower end is when I'm adjusting the weight.

The notebook loses on speed. It wins on battery life and it never asks you to update to iOS 19.

But if you want history, PRs, a rest timer that follows you out of the app, and the ability to actually read your own numbers three months from now — that's what Unit does, at roughly the same speed as opening a Moleskine.

Free on App Store.

### C1 — Why Ghost Values Destroy Progression Engines

**Length:** ~55 seconds | **Word count:** 141 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~56 seconds

**Visual notes:** Screen recording showing ghost-value pre-fill. Then optionally a side-by-side: competitor app showing a "suggested weight" vs Unit showing last actual weight. Let the contrast speak.

---

Every progression engine assumes your last session was accurate. If you logged 100 kilograms but you actually used 95 because the plates were wrong, the engine sends you to 102.5 next week. It's working from bad data.

Ghost values don't suggest anything. They show you what you logged — which, if you're logging accurately, is exactly what you lifted.

The difference is: a progression engine is a model of your training. A ghost value is a mirror of your training. The model can be wrong. The mirror just shows what's there.

When you look at 100 kilograms pre-filled before your next bench press set, you already know whether to match it, exceed it, or back off. You don't need an algorithm for that. You need accurate history and a second to think.

Ghost values give you the history. The decision is yours.

**Length:** ~55 seconds | **Word count:** 140 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~56 seconds

**Visual notes:** Screen recording of Unit's clean set log — no recommendations, no targets. Optionally briefly show a competitor's target-vs-actual UI (Hevy or Strong) to show the contrast, then return to Unit.

### C4 — Why Bodyweight Exercises Shouldn't Show "0 kg"

**Length:** ~40 seconds | **Word count:** 102 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~41 seconds

**Visual notes:** Screen recording showing a bodyweight exercise — pull-ups or dips — displaying "BW" instead of a weight field. Clean, clear, no ambiguity. Cut to the history view showing BW logged correctly across sessions.

---

"0 kg" for a pull-up is not neutral. It's a claim that the exercise weighs nothing, which is obviously false, and it breaks the data model for anyone who tracks weighted pull-ups alongside bodyweight pull-ups — suddenly you can't distinguish them in history.

The right answer is "BW." Bodyweight. It's not a placeholder, it's a value. It tells you exactly what the load was: your own bodyweight, unweighted.

When you add a 10-kilogram plate to a dip, that logs as "+10 kg." The two variants are distinguishable in history. Neither of them says zero.

Small thing. But your PR detection, your history read, and your own memory of the session all work better when the data is honest.

### C5 — The Math of "Seconds Per Set Logged"

**Length:** ~55 seconds | **Word count:** 136 | **Duration at 150 wpm:** ~54 seconds

**Visual notes:** Simple overlay text showing the math: 16 sets × N seconds = total logging time. Show two versions — competitor app at 5 seconds per set, Unit at 2 seconds. Then screen recording of a real set being logged with a timer overlay.

---

A typical session is sixteen sets. Maybe twenty on a big day.

If logging each set takes six seconds — which is about where Hevy lands when you're entering weight and reps manually — that's ninety-six seconds of interaction time per session. Not counting the times you fumble the number field and have to re-enter.

If it takes two seconds, that's thirty-two seconds. Across five sessions a week, that's three minutes saved. Over a year of training, around two and a half hours.

That math matters for a different reason than efficiency: friction compounds. Every extra tap is one more moment where you consider whether it's worth opening the app. At six seconds, the app starts to feel like work. At two seconds, it disappears.

The app should disappear. That's the design goal. You should forget you're using it.

The metric is seconds per set logged. Everything else is secondary.

## How to Use These in ElevenLabs

### Voice settings

| Setting | Value | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Stability | ~50 | Prevents the voice from sounding too robotic (high stability) or too variable (low). Midpoint gives the "dry narrator" quality that matches Unit's brand voice. |
| Clarity / Similarity boost | ~75 | High enough to stay recognizable; lower than max avoids the over-crisp "AI voice" artifact. |
| Style exaggeration | 0 | Leave at zero. Unit's voice is flat and direct. Any style boost pushes toward expressive/upbeat, which breaks the brand register. |
| Speaker boost | Off | Only needed if the source voice recording is quiet. If your training audio is well-recorded, leave it off. |

### Timing and sync tips

- **Generate audio first, then sync to video** — not the other way around. ElevenLabs output length is hard to predict precisely. Generate, note the runtime, then trim your screen recording to match.
- **Leave 0.3–0.5s of silence before the first word** — gives you room to sync the audio start to the first action on screen without clipping the opening consonant.
- **Pause marks in the script:** natural sentence breaks are your sync points. Where a cut or a visual transition happens on screen, that's where a period (pause) should fall in the script. Rewrite sentences if needed to put pauses at the right moments.
- **For comparison scripts (D1–D3):** the scripts are written so the narration matches left-side action first, then right-side action. Time the screen recording to match this rhythm — left side finishes, pause, right side starts — so the audio never runs ahead of the visual.
- **Silences at the end:** the closing lines ("Faster than paper. Free on App Store.") are designed as two short beats. Let them land with a half-second gap between them. In ElevenLabs, a period creates a natural pause; if you want more space, use a comma or break the sentences across two separate generations.

### Slop-avoidance check (per `elevenlabs-protocol.md`)

Before you export: confirm the visual track is real screen recording of the app, not stock footage, AI-generated visuals, or mockups. The allowed combination is your voice clone + your real UI in motion. Any other combination trips the TikTok/IG fitness slop filter.

### Iteration workflow

Scripts are meant to be edited. If a sentence sounds unnatural in the generated audio, cut it or rewrite it. The best voiceover is the one that sounds like you actually said it — these scripts are starting points, not final copy.

If generated audio for a script consistently sounds over-enunciated or stilted, reduce sentence length. Shorter sentences with real periods give ElevenLabs clearer prosodic structure to work from.
